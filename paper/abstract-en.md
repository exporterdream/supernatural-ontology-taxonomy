# Abstract: Supernatural Ontology Taxonomy — The Bi-Axial Model of Source and Dimension

**Author**: Zihan Tian 
**Date**: 2026-06-26  
**Keywords**: Supernatural Ontology, Monster Taxonomy, Folklore Studies, ACGN, Bi-Axial Model, Cultural Translation

## Abstract

Traditional folklore classifications of supernatural beings remain coherent within their original cultural contexts, yet they falter when applied to cross-cultural comparison or to entities emerging from modern narrative forms such as web literature, analog horror, and rule-based creepypasta. As Cohen (1996) argues, the "monster" is fundamentally a cultural construct—a projection of the Other onto the boundaries of human cognition. But when those boundaries shift across time, geography, and media, traditional taxonomies become inadequate.

This paper proposes a **Bi-Axial Model** to address this gap. The model locates any supernatural entity along two independent axes:

**Axis 1 — The Source (S)**: ontological origin, coded into six categories:
- S-Nat (Nature/Essence)
- S-Soul (Psyche/Karma/Votive Faith)
- S-Bio (Biology/Virus/Infection)
- S-Cos (Cosmic/Extra-dimensional)
- S-Rule (Rule/Code/Protocol)
- S-Concept (Collective Narrative/Cognitive Consensus)

**Axis 2 — The Dimension (D)**: mode of manifestation, coded into five states:
- D-0 (Pure Ethereal)
- D-1 (Semi-corporeal)
- D-2 (Solid Physical)
- D-3 (Avatar/Projection)
- D-Info (Informational: existing as data, text, or cognitive pattern)

Every entity is defined by the formula **Entity = (Source, Dimension)**.

The model is demonstrated through exemplary cases from Chinese classical literature (*Strange Tales*, *Journey to the West*), Western Gothic literature (*Dracula*, Lovecraft's Mythos), modern cinema (Romero's zombies, *Mr. Vampire*), and emerging digital narratives (Mandela Catalogue, rule-based horror, *Incantation*). These cases are illustrative rather than exhaustive—the framework is designed to accommodate any supernatural entity from any cultural tradition.

The paper argues that confusion in supernatural classification arises from the **cultural translation** (Asad, 1986) and **semantic drift** (Ullmann, 1962) of labels as they migrate across cultural contexts and narrative genres. The Bi-Axial Model offers a universal tool for scholars, writers, and world-builders to precisely locate any supernatural being on a clear, extensible coordinate system—while respecting the internal coherence of each tradition and the fluidity of its meanings across time.
